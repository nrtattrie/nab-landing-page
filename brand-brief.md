# Nab Brand Brief

## Purpose

This document captures the current brand direction for Nab so product, marketing, screenshots, website, copy, and future design work all point toward the same aesthetic. It is not a forever-locked identity document. It is the current working brief for pre-launch decisions.

## Core idea

Nab is a smart, tasteful shortcut for people who are tired of hunting for restaurant reservations.

It should feel like a better way into a good restaurant night, not like a gimmick, a luxury concierge club, or a bargain-hacker tool.

## Positioning

Nab helps users get into places they actually want without requiring them to perform status, obsess over the hunt, or grind through search-first reservation workflows.

The app is inclusive, but it gives users a way to sneak into something that often feels exclusive.

## Audience read

The target user is not "everyone who eats out." The target user is closer to:

- city-based
- already aware of OpenTable and Resy
- values ease and taste at the same time
- wants anticipation and discovery, but not childish gamification
- wants a clean product they do not feel embarrassed to use

## Brand personality

Nab should feel like:

- a smart friend with taste
- lightly wry
- urban
- warm
- restaurant-first
- approachable, but higher-end
- helpful
- anticipatory

Nab should not feel like:

- a parody of Tinder
- a luxury velvet-rope club
- a loud delivery app
- a goofy cartoon food app
- a cold utility dashboard
- a "cheeky shortcut" in a cheap or hacky sense

## Emotional target

Primary emotion:

- anticipation

Secondary emotions:

- relief
- confidence
- satisfaction

The feeling should be: "I found something good without the usual nonsense."

## Tone of voice

Recommended tone:

- concise
- smart
- helpful
- slightly wry
- warm

Avoid:

- over-joking
- dating-app innuendo
- snark
- faux-luxury copy
- startup-marketing hype

## Aesthetic direction

### Overall

The visual direction should be clean and modern with warmth.

This is not a dark, moody nightlife brand. It also is not a bright fast-casual food app. The sweet spot is tasteful restaurant-world warmth with urban clarity.

### Visual world

The app should feel more rooted in restaurants than nightlife generally.

Reference mood:

- West Village more than Midtown
- desirable neighborhood restaurant more than private club
- warm hospitality more than exclusivity theater

### Layout

Prioritize:

- clean graphic structure
- selective imagery
- breathing room
- strong hierarchy
- calm transitions

Avoid:

- clutter
- decorative filler
- image overload
- trying to "sell the app" like a startup landing page

## Recommended visual system

### Color

Direction:

- warm base
- food-adjacent accent
- restrained supporting darks

Current recommendation:

- warm cream / soft ivory
- tomato / coral / red-orange accent
- deep brown or burgundy for grounding

Use dark tones as support, not the dominant emotional read.

Avoid:

- overly dark luxury palettes as the main system
- generic ketchup-and-mustard saturation everywhere
- one-note red/yellow fast-food energy

### Typography

Direction:

- clean modern sans
- friendly enough to feel human
- not bubbly
- not fashion-editorial as the main voice

Use typography to convey clarity and confidence, not irony or prestige.

### Shape language

Direction:

- rounded, but not toy-like
- soft corners
- clear silhouettes

The product should feel tactile and approachable without becoming soft or childish.

### Imagery

Direction:

- selective food and restaurant imagery
- crisp, appetizing, real
- use sparingly

Avoid:

- stocky hero imagery
- generic nightlife mood boards
- excessive overhead food glamour
- visual noise in every screen

## Icon and logo guidance

Current accepted direction:

- tilted-card `N` mark

Why it works:

- ties to the swipe/deck mechanic
- simple enough to function as an app icon
- distinctive enough for pre-launch

What it should avoid:

- extra decorative marks
- overly literal reservation UI details
- noisy backgrounds

This direction is accepted for now even though it may evolve later.

## Mascot guidance

Mascot is optional and should remain secondary.

If Nab uses a character, it should behave like a clever helper, not a front-and-center cartoon mascot.

Good direction:

- subtle, ownable, minimalist
- suggests finding, scouting, or helping

Avoid:

- humans
- maître d' characters
- imps
- direct table references that drift toward OpenTable territory
- animals with dirty/gross associations

Potential secondary directions worth exploring later:

- squirrel
- swallow/swift

## Motion guidance

Motion should be:

- clean
- nuanced
- responsive
- low-drama

The current recommended motion philosophy is "subtle reward":

- crisp gesture-linked swipes
- restrained entrance/exit motion
- gentle confirmation moments
- haptics for intent, warning, and success

Avoid:

- constant playful animation
- over-bouncy movement
- celebratory overload

## Product translation

### App UI

Should feel:

- polished
- warm
- easy
- quietly elegant

### Website

Should feel:

- useful and credible
- not like a venture-backed landing page
- aligned with the app's clean, tasteful restaurant-world energy

### App Store screenshots

Should emphasize:

- ease
- anticipation
- good taste
- clean structure

Not:

- gimmick
- chaos
- novelty for novelty's sake

## Three practical visual directions

### Direction A — Warm Utility

Best for:

- app UI
- product screenshots
- onboarding

Characteristics:

- cream-heavy base
- tomato/coral accents
- deep brown support
- clean sans
- simple iconography

Risk:

- can become too generic if not sharpened by strong structure and copy

### Direction B — Restaurant Editorial Lite

Best for:

- website
- app store screenshots
- occasional marketing assets

Characteristics:

- warm neutrals
- elegant spacing
- selective food/interior photography
- slightly more refined typographic rhythm

Risk:

- can drift too premium or precious if pushed too far

### Direction C — Clever Helper

Best for:

- mascot system
- empty states
- secondary marketing moments

Characteristics:

- minimalist helper energy
- subtle personality
- supports the product instead of dominating it

Risk:

- can get cute fast if not tightly restrained

## Decision filters

Before approving a visual or marketing choice, ask:

1. Does this feel like a smart friend with taste?
2. Does this make the product easier to trust and easier to use?
3. Does this feel warm and restaurant-first rather than dark, gimmicky, or luxury-coded?
4. Would both a casual user and a taste-conscious snob still feel comfortable using this?
5. Is this helping Nab feel more ownable, or just more decorated?

If the answer to 1, 2, 3, and 4 is not clearly yes, the choice is probably off-brief.
