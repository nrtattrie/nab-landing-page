# Nab Website Direction

This document translates the Nab brand brief and brand system into a concrete direction for the public website / landing page.

It is intentionally practical. The goal is to make implementation decisions easier, not to produce abstract brand language.

## 1. Website purpose

The Nab website exists to do five things:

1. give the app a credible public home
2. explain the app clearly in under a minute
3. route people to the App Store
4. provide support, feedback, and legal links
5. feel aligned with the app’s taste level without turning into a "startup site"

This is not a storytelling-heavy venture-backed homepage.

## 2. The website should feel like

- useful
- warm
- urban
- tasteful
- restaurant-first
- calm
- concise

It should not feel like:

- a glossy startup promo page
- a luxury-membership club
- a generic food delivery site
- a loud social app

## 3. Core message

Primary message:

Nab helps users find verified-available restaurant reservations without the usual search hunt.

Supporting message:

It is a better, calmer, more tasteful way to discover a table you actually want.

## 4. Best website format

Recommended format:

- one-page site
- light, warm visual system
- selective use of screenshots and imagery
- simple navigation

Recommended top-level sections:

1. hero
2. how it works
3. why Nab feels different
4. screenshot strip
5. FAQ
6. feedback + support
7. legal footer

## 5. Hero direction

### Job

The hero should immediately answer:

- what the app is
- why it is useful
- what the user should do next

### Hero composition

Recommended structure:

- simple headline on the left or centered
- app mockup or selective restaurant/food image on the right or below
- App Store CTA
- small supporting line under the headline

### Headline zone

Good headline directions:

- Nab a great reservation without the hunt.
- Swipe verified tables, not endless search results.
- Find a table worth saying yes to.

Do not write a headline that sounds:

- too clever
- too jokey
- too luxury-coded
- too much like a dating app

### Supporting copy

Good supporting copy should explain:

- Nab shows tables that are actually available
- the user swipes on options instead of digging through search results
- booking still finishes through OpenTable

## 6. Page sections

### Section 1 — Hero

Goal:

- clarity + desirability

Needed elements:

- logo
- one-line value proposition
- brief explainer
- App Store CTA
- optional secondary CTA for feedback/support

### Section 2 — How it works

Goal:

- make the concept obvious and credible

Recommended 3-step flow:

1. pick your date, time, and party
2. swipe through tables that are actually available
3. save and book the one you want

Visual style:

- simple cards or columns
- icon-supported, but not over-illustrated

### Section 3 — Why Nab feels different

Goal:

- separate Nab from search-first reservation apps

Possible points:

- availability first
- less hunting
- more discovery
- fewer dead ends

Tone:

- practical
- confident
- not combative

### Section 4 — Screenshots

Goal:

- make the product feel real and polished

Recommended screenshot sequence:

1. discover / swipe deck
2. restaurant card detail
3. matches / saved reservations
4. booking flow

Visual treatment:

- generous spacing
- warm background
- screenshot frames or device mockups only if clean

### Section 5 — FAQ

Goal:

- answer obvious hesitation fast

Suggested questions:

- What is Nab?
- Are the reservations actually available?
- How do bookings work?
- Do I need OpenTable?
- Where is Nab available?

### Section 6 — Feedback and support

Goal:

- make the site feel alive and trustworthy

Should include:

- support email
- feedback board link
- short invitation to share bugs / restaurant requests / thoughts

### Section 7 — Footer

Must include:

- Privacy Policy
- Terms of Service
- support email
- copyright / brand

## 7. Visual direction

### Backgrounds

The site should be lighter and warmer than the current app UI.

Best direction:

- cream-forward backgrounds
- warm section changes
- one or two darker accents only where needed

### Accent usage

Use tomato/coral for:

- CTAs
- small highlights
- badges
- section emphasis

Use brown/burgundy for:

- text hierarchy
- footer grounding
- subtle framing

### Cards and sections

- sections should feel editorial and breathable
- cards should be used for repeated units, screenshots, FAQs, and small info clusters
- avoid stacking too many nested cards

## 8. Imagery

### Best image types

- warm restaurant interiors
- tablescape moments
- close but not overly styled food photography
- polished app screenshots

### What to avoid

- random stock people laughing at dinner
- dark blurry cocktail photos
- excessive overhead food shots
- generic startup abstract art

### Image rule

Every image should either:

- help the user understand the app, or
- deepen the restaurant-world mood

If it does neither, do not use it.

## 9. Copy direction

### Tone

- concise
- useful
- smart
- lightly wry

### Good copy traits

- short sentences
- direct claims
- little friction in the language
- enough taste to feel intentional

### Avoid

- "revolutionizing dining"
- "AI-powered discovery for modern food lovers"
- excessive "exclusive" language
- heavy dating metaphors
- startup chest-thumping

## 10. Navigation

Keep it simple.

Recommended nav:

- How it Works
- Screenshots
- FAQ
- Feedback
- Download

Do not overbuild multi-page navigation unless the site later grows meaningfully.

## 11. Conversion logic

Primary CTA:

- Download on the App Store

Secondary CTA:

- Join TestFlight / Get updates / Leave feedback

The site should always make the next step obvious without feeling pushy.

## 12. Mobile behavior

Because most Nab users will first encounter this on mobile:

- headline must fit comfortably
- CTA should appear early
- hero should reveal enough of the next section below the fold
- screenshots should stay legible without feeling tiny

## 13. What the website should not try to do

Do not make the site:

- a full restaurant editorial magazine
- a huge brand statement
- a parody of dating-app culture
- dark for the sake of looking cool
- overly animated

## 14. Implementation note

If the site is built soon, it should follow the current pre-launch brand direction:

- warm, lighter than the app
- clean graphic structure
- selective imagery
- simple CTA path

If there is any tension between "looks impressive" and "feels clear, tasteful, and usable," choose the latter.
