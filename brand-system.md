# Nab Brand System

This document turns the current brand brief into concrete guidance for palette, typography, website direction, and App Store screenshots.

Use it when making future UI, website, screenshot, illustration, and marketing decisions.

## 1. Brand summary

Nab should feel like:

- a smart friend with taste
- warm, not cold
- restaurant-first
- urban but not exclusive
- approachable, but still elegant
- lightly wry, not flippant
- anticipatory, not frantic

Nab should not feel like:

- a dating-app parody
- a dark velvet-rope luxury brand
- a bright fast-food app
- a jokey startup
- a bargain-hacker tool

## 2. Palette recommendation

### Core palette

Use this as the primary working direction for future brand and marketing materials.

| Role | Color | Hex | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Base cream | Soft Ivory | `#F7EEDC` | Warm, edible, inviting; use for light surfaces, screenshot backgrounds, section bands |
| Primary accent | Tomato Coral | `#E85B4A` | Main brand accent; energetic without reading as fast food |
| Secondary accent | Red Orange | `#F1784B` | Use sparingly for gradient warmth, emphasis, badges |
| Grounding dark | Cocoa Brown | `#4A3028` | Text on light backgrounds, framing, subtle depth |
| Supporting dark | Burgundy Brown | `#6D3A34` | Accent depth, dividers, darker chips, screenshot grounding |
| Soft neutral | Toast | `#D8C0A4` | Borders, muted pills, secondary fills |
| Utility success | Herb Green | `#5E9B72` | Success only; do not treat as a brand color |
| Utility warning | Brick | `#C75A45` | Warnings/errors only; should harmonize with the primary accent |

### How to use the palette

- Let cream do more work than black.
- Let tomato coral be the main energy color.
- Use brown/burgundy to ground the system rather than starting from a dark screen.
- Keep green limited to success states.
- Use gradients only sparingly, mostly for marketing surfaces or image treatment, not everywhere in product UI.

### What to avoid

- black-first or charcoal-first brand expression
- equal amounts of red and yellow on every screen
- neon saturation
- generic delivery-app color energy
- luxury black/gold as the dominant identity

## 3. Typography recommendation

### Primary type direction

Use a clean modern sans with a friendly edge.

The type should feel:

- crisp
- readable
- contemporary
- human

It should not feel:

- bubbly
- fashion-editorial
- overly geometric/cold
- whimsical

### Practical recommendation

For product and screenshots:

- keep using a clean system sans if needed
- if you later adopt a custom type system, choose something in the zone of:
  - modern grotesk / humanist sans
  - slightly softened terminals
  - strong legibility in app UI

### Typographic behavior

- headlines: concise and confident
- subheads: explanatory, not poetic
- body: compact and readable
- labels/buttons: clean and restrained, avoid shouting

### What to avoid

- editorial serif as the main identity
- overly quirky rounded fonts
- heavy all-caps branding
- too much tiny light-gray text

## 4. Shape and component direction

### Shapes

- rounded corners, but not toy-like
- cards should feel tactile and polished
- avoid overly sharp enterprise angles
- avoid oversized bubbly pills everywhere

### Components

- cards should feel restaurant/editorial-adjacent, not generic SaaS tiles
- pills and chips should be warm and legible
- buttons should feel intentional, not ornamental
- keep decoration light

## 5. Imagery direction

### Use

Use selective imagery, not image overload.

Best image categories:

- appetizing but realistic dishes
- warm restaurant interiors
- tablescape details
- candid restaurant atmosphere with clarity

### Image treatment

- warm color temperature
- enough contrast to feel rich
- avoid overly dark, muddy imagery
- avoid generic stock-style overhead food boards

### Role of imagery

Imagery should support appetite and desirability. It should not do all of the branding work by itself.

## 6. Website art direction

### Website job

The website is not a startup billboard. It is a credible, tasteful public face for Nab.

It should:

- explain the app clearly
- feel aligned with the product
- support App Review / launch credibility
- route users to App Store, support, legal, and feedback

### Website mood

Best direction:

- warm utility
- restaurant editorial lite
- clean graphic design with selective imagery

### Homepage structure

Recommended structure:

1. Hero
2. How it works
3. Why Nab feels different
4. Screenshots
5. FAQ
6. Feedback / support / legal links

### Hero direction

- use real restaurant/food imagery or a polished app mockup, not abstract gradients
- headline should be simple and literal
- supporting copy can carry the taste/personality

Good headline zone:

- "Nab a great reservation without the hunt."
- "Swipe verified tables, not endless search results."

### Website visual rules

- cream or very light warm backgrounds should do most of the work
- coral/tomato accent for CTAs and emphasis
- brown/burgundy for grounding text or section contrast
- use dark sections only as punctuation, not the whole site
- avoid making the site feel darker than the app’s brand direction

## 7. App Store screenshot style guide

### Screenshot goal

The screenshots should make Nab feel:

- easier
- smarter
- warmer
- more desirable

They should not try to entertain on their own.

### Core message sequence

Recommended 5-screen narrative:

1. Find verified tables fast
2. Swipe instead of hunting
3. See the restaurant clearly
4. Save and manage reservations simply
5. Book through OpenTable with less friction

### Composition rules

- use clean framing with strong hierarchy
- one idea per screenshot
- large, readable headline
- limited supporting copy
- show real app UI, not fake mockups that oversell
- keep the background warm and uncluttered

### Visual style

- cream-forward backgrounds
- one accent color per screenshot
- subtle use of food/interior imagery if needed
- use the tilted-card icon only when it helps, not as a watermark on everything

### Headline style

Good screenshot headlines are:

- direct
- useful
- compact

Examples:

- "Only see tables that are actually available"
- "Swipe your way to something good"
- "Save the ones worth coming back to"

Avoid:

- jokey dating-app references
- luxury-club language
- too much slang

## 8. Mascot direction

Mascot, if pursued later, should remain secondary.

### Good role

- empty states
- celebration moments
- website accents
- onboarding/marketing sidecar

### Bad role

- replacing the icon
- dominating the UI
- becoming the whole brand personality

### Good directions

- clever helper
- quick scout
- minimalist line-based animal silhouette

### Candidate animals

- squirrel: urban, quick, finder/forager energy
- swallow/swift: fast, elegant, clean silhouette

### Avoid

- animals with dirty associations
- overly cute mascots
- humanized hosts/maitre d' figures

## 9. Product rules derived from the brand

When making future UI choices:

- make it warmer before making it flashier
- make it cleaner before making it more expressive
- make it more restaurant-first before making it more nightlife-coded
- make it more trustworthy before making it more clever

## 10. Current implementation note

The current in-app visual system still leans darker than the long-term brand recommendation in the brief.

That is acceptable for now.

Treat the current app as the working pre-launch baseline, and use this document as the guide for future convergence across:

- website
- screenshots
- next-round UI refinements
- brand assets

Do not force a large visual rewrite immediately unless there is a clear product reason to do it.
